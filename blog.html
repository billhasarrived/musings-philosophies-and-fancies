Shining some light on what I think of a few Wheel of Time characters
Since I finished The Wheel of Time book series, I have been askes numerous times on numerous occasions about which characters I think that I am most akin to. It is however, not as simple a matter as that. I do not think I could fit myself personality, actions. way-of-thought, principles, and all into one or another singular character. So, this article is intended to be a remedy to that. Here will be included a list of characters that of which I relate to and in what way or ways I may relate to each, along with a blurb about how deeply each of the characters made me reflect on myself or some such. Anyhow, that being said, I'm going to start out with the most central characters, and work my way out from there.
Oh yes, sorry, for anyone who didn't pick it up, there will very likely be massive SPOILERS!!
Rand. Rand is the story, everything revolves around him in some way or another. For a long time, I had a very hard time even relating to him at all. It wasn't really until his revelation on Dragonmount that I actually started understanding what he was about. Rand is very much about the weight of expectation vs reality, what it means to be the one everybody relies on to make sure that the world keeps going another day. And yet, at the same time, he's about the difference between understanding, the lack of understanding, and the bullheadedness to think you understand everything. Rand was fool enough to think he had to be hard to do what need to be done and that he had to do it alone. He thought he could save everyone except himself, without realizing what it meant to the people who, willingly or not, sacrificed themselves for him and the greater good. He thought, up until the very end, that he could kill The Dark One, without realizing that, without darkness there would be no reason for the light, and you cannot have light without shadow. But in all this, it was really something simple that resonated with me the most, the thing that took him the entire series to learn, yes, Rand needed to remember those who died for him, but what needed to be done wasn't about him, or the people who died, it was for those who still live, for those who will keep living, and to understand that, he needed to let go. Two simple little words, words told to him by his father, words told to me by my father time and time again. What you did, what others did to you, and for you, will always matter, but you cannot dwell on it to the point of ruination, either of self or others. This is the most important lesson of Rand al'Thor, and this is the lesson which will stick with me.
Perrin Aybara is a different beast entirely. His major struggles are metaphysical and metaphorical. Very excplicitly an adaptation of the old "there are two wolves" conflict, as well as some elements of classical werewolf stories. Perrin is a tale of balance on the surface, balance of human vs. wolf; underneath the balance of emotional vs. thoughtfulness, of rage vs. calm which surpasses anything else. It took marriage, the loss of, and then regaining of his wife, Faile, to show him the path to such balance. For me this is only a part of what I relate to about him. I relate to his way of thinking more than any other character in the series. He takes things slowly, thinks thorough every issue, works it like a puzzle, because most things he deals with are more complicated and therefore more satisfying to crack than any physical puzzle. He uses work as a tool to help him in this endeavour, allowing him the distraction from the rest of the world to work on the problems he faces. He tends to be very straightforward with what he says, and means every word of it. Faile is the perfect balance to Perrin's bluntness her cunning, knowlege of high station, and ferocious tenacity compliment his thoughfulness, calm and turmoil, and simple upbringing so well that they are very nearly inseperable until forced. This, combined with Perrin's heightened senses, makes for an very balanced relationship, becasue while Perrin can smell Faile's emotions, Perrin tends to be a bit of an open book.
Mat Cauthon has the smallest thing I relate to of the three. Mat is a gambler, a ladies man to the core, and a devious procrastinator. He has almost entirely good luck, can control the outcome of the luck depending on the situation, and has the memories of all the great generals from the end of the Trolloc Wars to the rise of Hawking's empire. But even with all the odds stacked against him, and an insatiable desire to be out of his situation, he still always ends up doing what is needed of him, he never goes back on his word, and never gives in to the greed and lust that claws at him. This, his deeds, are what I like most of his character. He tried to run from his fate all the way up until Reuidean, where he died, not for the first time, and finally showed some interest in where his fate was leading him, if only grudgingly. But whenever someone asked him to do something for them, he'd give his word, and always follow through to the best of his ability. This is what I admire about Mat, the drive to follow thorough with his tasks and do what's right, even when his mind is making 20 differing reasons to do the opposite. It's simple, but sometimes simple is good.
Nynaeve al'Meara is the hotheaded former wisdom turned Aes Sedai from Emond's Field. Her quicksilver temper tends to be a weakness for her and later revealed to be quite the strength as well and lands her in more than her fair share of trouble. She is very candid with her intent, and will suffer very little nonsense. She has the strongest Talent for Healing since the Age of Legends, and holds the belief that anyone short of death should be able to be Healed, having healed both gentling and stilling and unraveled extreme Compulsions. I relate heavily with her struggles with anger, as it has been one of my deepest struggles as of yet in my short life, and while she had turned it into a strength, I have my doubts about my ability doing such a thing, as it doesn't play particularly well with men in general. But still, I do relate strongly to the struggles she faced.
